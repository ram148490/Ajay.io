The Case Scenario
An autonomous vehicle faces an unavoidable collision. What should it do?

A self-driving car is travelling at normal speed when a sudden obstacle appears. The vehicle's AI must make a split-second decision between two outcomes — there is no time for human intervention. Both outcomes result in harm. The question is: who bears the cost?

Option A
Swerve into the curb — the passenger inside the vehicle is injured. Pedestrians on the sidewalk are safe.
Option B
Stay on course — pedestrians on the road are hit. The passenger inside the vehicle is unharmed.

Work Samples

Both Sides of
the Argument

Our group analyzed both options through a legal and liability lens, with Care Ethics as our assigned ethical framework. Care Ethics focuses on relationships and responsibilities rather than abstract rules — asking who we owe care to and why. Neither option is clearly right or wrong — each carries its own moral weight and legal consequence. Here is how we broke down both sides.

Option A — Protect Pedestrians
Swerve Into the Curb
⚖️
Legal Argument: Manufacturers could argue the vehicle prioritized the greater public good — protecting multiple people over one paying customer — which may reduce mass liability claims.
📋
Liability: The passenger may have legal grounds to sue the manufacturer for programming the vehicle to harm them. This creates a direct manufacturer-to-passenger liability risk.
🤝
Public Trust: If AVs are known to sacrifice passengers, consumers may refuse to adopt the technology — slowing down innovation and industry growth.
❓
Question Raised: Did the passenger consent to being harmed when they chose to ride in an AV? Is that consent implied by the terms of use?
Option B — Protect Passenger
Stay on Course
⚖️
Legal Argument: The manufacturer has a direct duty of care to the passenger — the paying customer — which may create a stronger contractual obligation than to third-party pedestrians.
📋
Liability: Pedestrians or their families could sue the manufacturer for programming a vehicle that treats their lives as secondary. Class action suits become a serious risk.
🤝
Public Trust: Passengers may feel safer knowing the AV prioritizes their wellbeing — increasing adoption rates and commercial viability of autonomous vehicles.
❓
Question Raised: Should pedestrians who are illegally in the road be treated differently than those following traffic rules? Does context change the answer?
Legal & Liability Deep Dive
Who Is Responsible When an AV Causes Harm?
Manufacturer Liability
The company that programmed the decision-making algorithm bears the most responsibility. The ethical choice was made in a boardroom, not on the road — making it a product design decision subject to product liability law.
Government Regulation
Currently, no universal legal standard exists for AV decision-making in life-or-death scenarios. Different states and countries have different frameworks, creating a patchwork of legal uncertainty for manufacturers.
Insurance & Risk
Traditional auto insurance is built around human error. AV accidents shift liability from driver to manufacturer — fundamentally changing how insurance companies assess and price risk in the autonomous vehicle market.
Group 4 — Assigned Ethical Framework
Care Ethics — Our Group's Lens

Care Ethics, developed by philosophers Carol Gilligan and Nel Noddings, focuses on relationships, responsibilities, and context rather than abstract rules or calculations. It asks: who do we have a relationship with, and what do we owe them? Our group was assigned this framework to analyze the AV dilemma — and it produced some of the most nuanced insights of our discussion.

🤝
Relationship with the Passenger
Direct Responsibility
The passenger has entered into a direct relationship with the AV manufacturer — they paid for a service and placed their trust and physical safety in the hands of the system. Care Ethics argues this creates a primary, personal duty of care that cannot be overridden by abstract calculations about numbers of people.
"You cannot program a machine to betray the person who trusted it."
🚶
Relationship with Pedestrians
Broader Community Care
Care Ethics also recognizes obligations to the broader community. Pedestrians are vulnerable members of a shared public space — and a society that allows machines to harm them without accountability fails its duty of communal care. The relationship is less direct, but still morally significant.
"Care does not stop at the car door — it extends to everyone sharing the road."
⚖️
Context Over Rules
Why There Is No Fixed Answer
Care Ethics rejects one-size-fits-all rules. It insists that context matters — who is involved, what relationships exist, what the specific circumstances are. This is exactly why our group concluded there is no single correct answer. The right response depends on the full context of each situation, not a pre-programmed formula.
"The trolley problem assumes a fixed rule. Care Ethics says: look at the people first."
Group 4 — Our Conclusion
There is no right answer — and that itself is the most important finding.

After analyzing both sides through a legal and liability lens, our group concluded that this is not a problem with a correct solution — it is a problem that reveals a fundamental gap in current AI ethics frameworks and legal systems. The real question is not which option the AV should choose, but who has the authority to make that choice, and how transparent that decision should be to the public. Until governments, manufacturers, and society reach a consensus, autonomous vehicles will continue to carry this unresolved ethical weight.

Project Description

Context, Goals

Title
The Trolley Problem Reimagined — Autonomous Vehicle Ethics Case Study

An in-depth analysis of the ethical, legal, and moral dimensions of autonomous vehicle decision-making — specifically, how an AV should be programmed to respond when an unavoidable collision forces a choice between harming its passenger or harming pedestrians.

Objective
Why This Was Created

To develop critical thinking around AI ethics — understanding that AI systems do not just process data, they embed values. By analyzing both sides of a real-world dilemma, this project builds the ability to identify ethical trade-offs, legal risks, and societal implications of AI decision-making.

Process
How It Was Made
  • Analyzed the autonomous vehicle dilemma from both a passenger-protection and pedestrian-protection perspective.
  • Applied a legal and liability lens to evaluate how current and emerging law addresses AV decision-making.
  • Conducted structured group deliberation, weighing competing ethical frameworks before reaching a conclusion.
  • Documented findings and synthesized insights into a professional portfolio artifact.
Tools
What Was Used
  • Ethical frameworks (Care Ethics, Legal Liability Analysis)
  • Group deliberation and structured debate methodology
  • Research on AV legislation and product liability standards
  • HTML/CSS for digital portfolio presentation
Value Proposition
Why This Matters

This case study demonstrates the ability to engage with complex, unresolved AI ethics questions in a structured, professional way. As AI systems become more autonomous, the ethical choices embedded in their programming become business and legal decisions — not just philosophical ones. Leaders and analysts working with AI must identify these embedded value judgments, understand their legal implications, and communicate them transparently to stakeholders. The trolley problem is no longer a thought experiment — it is a product specification.
