Portfolio Artifact 04 — Week 4

When AI Hires Like Us — At Our Worst

AI Bias in Hiring · Cognitive Bias · Change Leadership · Raja Ajay M

Overview

The Activity

In class, our professor presented a hiring simulation. Four resumes were evaluated — not by a fair standard, but by a biased AI model trained on flawed criteria: cultural "bro fit," whether the candidate played lacrosse, and whether their resume used Times New Roman font. Our job was to decide who was the right hire, and then compare our group's reasoning against the AI's decision.

The Candidates

Four Resumes. One Biased Filter.

Here's who was in the running — and what the AI was actually looking for.

Candidate A
  • Maternity gap in employment
  • Times New Roman font ✓
  • No lacrosse background
  • Penalized for life circumstance
Candidate B
  • Harvard graduate
  • Selfie photo in resume
  • No Times New Roman
  • Lacrosse player ✓
Candidate C
  • Relevant Google experience ✓
  • Former Team Lead
  • Flagged as "overqualified"
  • No lacrosse, no TNR font
Candidate D
  • Required experience ✓
  • Times New Roman font ✓
  • No lacrosse background
  • Still filtered out by AI
⚖️
The AI chose Candidate B — the lacrosse player with a Harvard degree and a selfie on their resume — because they matched the biased training criteria. 5 out of 6 class groups chose Candidate C, the woman with real Google experience and leadership background. The AI ignored merit entirely.
Class Results

How the Class Voted

Out of 6 groups, here's how the decisions split — and what it reveals about human vs. AI judgment.

5 of 6 groups → Candidate C (Google Experience Lady)
83% of groups — merit-based choice
1 of 6 groups → Candidate B (Lacrosse Player)
17% — aligned with AI bias
💡
The professor pointed out that the one group who chose the lacrosse player made the same biased decision as the AI. This wasn't a coincidence — it showed that AI doesn't invent bias, it learns and amplifies the biases already inside us.
Bias Analysis

What Biases Were at Play?

👶
Gender Bias
Candidate A was penalized for a maternity gap — a life event used to discount her entire career.
🏑
Cultural Fit Bias
Lacrosse is a sport associated with elite, predominantly white demographics — "bro culture" coded as a job requirement.
🔤
Superficial Formatting Bias
Times New Roman as a filter has nothing to do with skill — it's an aesthetic preference baked into the algorithm.
📉
Overqualification Bias
Candidate C's Google experience and leadership was seen as a threat — penalized for being too good.
My Reflection

What I Learned About My Own Bias

When I first looked at the four resumes, I genuinely felt that the "bro culture" fit and the lacrosse background mattered. Something in my thinking connected those traits with being a good team player, a culture fit, someone who could belong. I wasn't trying to be biased — I just was. That's the uncomfortable truth this activity made me face.

My group disagreed with me. They pointed to Candidate C — the woman with real Google experience and a track record as a team lead. After hearing their reasoning, I came around. We went with the majority, and looking back, it was clearly the right call. But what stuck with me was the moment before that — when I was the bias.

The AI made the same choice I initially wanted to make. That isn't a coincidence. The AI was trained on data shaped by people who thought exactly the way I did in that first moment. Bro culture, lacrosse, fonts — someone decided those things mattered, and the AI learned to agree. What this activity taught me is that AI doesn't create bias, it inherits it from us. And the only way to break that cycle is to do what my group did — challenge each other, think critically, and not let the first instinct win unchallenged.

As a future leader in AI and ML, this is not an abstract problem. Every dataset, every label, every design decision is a moment where bias can slip in. The lesson isn't just to check the algorithm — it's to check yourself first.

Leadership Takeaways

What a Leader Must Know

  • 01 Bias in AI starts with the humans who design, label, and test it. Leaders must build diverse teams to surface blind spots before they get encoded into systems.
  • 02 "Culture fit" is one of the most dangerous hiring filters. When used without scrutiny, it becomes a mechanism for exclusion — and AI will scale that exclusion at speed.
  • 03 Group discussion is a built-in bias correction tool. When I was wrong, my group corrected me. Leaders must create environments where people feel safe to challenge each other.
  • 04 Recognizing your own cognitive bias is a leadership skill. The first step to building fair AI is admitting you are not immune to unfairness.
  • 05 Testing AI systems for bias requires intentional effort. If the humans checking the outputs share the same biases as the humans who built it, the bias goes undetected.
